Frands and Stena Petersen

great-great-grandparents of Sheldon Rampton

In an old black-and-white photograph, a dark-haired man with a full beard sits stiffly upright. He is a handsome man, with angular features and carefully groomed hair. He wears a dark shirt, a dark tie, and a white shirt -- a formal costume, showing the taking of this photograph to have been more than a casual event. You notice an unsmiling expression and the lack of flesh at his cheeks, but your attention comes back most often to his intensely earnest eyes, which stare out from beneath heavy, solid brows, their gaze traversing more than a century. And that is all we have of him, the patriarch: one image, one split-second in time preserved within the pages of an obscure family album, occasionally resurfacing.

Next to him, on the same page, is a portrait of his wife: a small, square, severe face, its hair tied back in a tight and temperate bun. A knobby nose. Obstinate, glaring eyes. Dark, definite lines around the mouth, as though she has spent most of her life clenching her jaws. She is the practical one of the two. You can imagine her preparing meals, disciplining children, managing each chore and transaction with the grim penurious determination of necessity. It is difficult, though, to imagine any romance in her. Her life is all struggle and survival. Her dress would look appropriate at any funeral ceremony, made of dark gathered material with a lace frill at the chest and some sort of ornament around the neck. Whether it is a ribbon or a broach you cannot tell, the photograph being to grainy for much detail.

The Mormon system of genealogical record-keepking provides a standardized system for assembling these small fragments from the past. Beneath the husband and wife, place pictures of the children, proceeding vertically from the oldest at the top to the youngest at the bottom. Next to each photograph appear the dates and locations for birth, marriage, death. By the sides of the father an mother appear the names of their respective parents. Each page thus becomes a record of three generations, documenting one link in a chain which in theory reaches back to the first parents themselves, Adam and Eve.

The album also contains a large photograph, from which the father is notably absent. It shows the entire family standing in front of what looks like a corral, each member dressed in his or her Sunday best. It is too blurred to show their faces clearly. Behind the posts and wooden poles of the corral are what appear to be three separate buildings. The one on the right stands considerably taller than the other two, and at the apex of its slanting roof a narrow sign is mounted, stretching from one end to the other. It bears his name and a quaintly misspelled advertisement: "F.P. PETERSEN -- DEALER OF MERCHANDIZE AND PRODUCTS."

This picture of my father (at left) was taken in the 1970s while he was touring as part of Elvis Presley's band. He remembered Elvis as a shy, easygoing man who kept to himself. Most of my father's career was spent as a musician playing for Las Vegas hotel show bands, but he toured a few times with traveling bands.
