Banned Books

Do you think censorship can't happen in the United States? Think again. The battle against those who would remove materials from libraries, schools, and bookstores continues, and in many areas has escalated. Here are some examples of books that have been challenged or banned. If you want to strike a blow for intellectual freedom and free speech, the best way to support a banned book is to buy it, read it, and share it with your friends!

Around the World in a Hundred Years
Henry the Navigator-Magellan
By Jean Fritz
Removed from the Carroll County, Md. schools (1995) because a passage on the burning of the library in Alexandria during the fourth century said that "Christians did not believe in scholarship" and mentioned intellectual suppression by Christians. "It's a sweeping generalization and it's definitely anti-Christian."

As I Lay Dying
By William Faulkner
This stream-of-consciousness novel, considered a literary masterpiece one from America's most gifted writers, was banned from the Graves County, Kentucky, school district in September 1986, on the grounds that it made reference to God and abortion in way that some people might find offensive.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

The Bastard
By John Jakes
Removed from Montour (Pennsylvania) High School library, 1976.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

The Bell Jar
By Sylvia Plath
This autobiograpical work eloquently tracks the author's descent into madness. Residents of Evansville, Illinois, challenged its use in school because it contains sexual material and advocates an "objectionable" philosophy.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry, Women.

Black Boy
By Richard Wright
Richard Wright's pathbreaking book about the black experience in the U.S. was removed, along with nine other titles, by Island Trees, New York, schools in 1976. The books were considered "immoral, anti-American, anti-Christian, or just plain filthy." The restriction was lifted by a ruling from the U.S. Supreme Court in 1982.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry, Race and Ethnicity.

Bloodline
By Sidney Sheldon
Challenged in Abingdon, Virginia, 1980; Challenged in Elizabethton, Tennessee, 1981.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

Brave New World
By Aldous Huxley
In this classic satire of a future world, all problems are solved through the use of "Soma," a fictional narcotic. Huxley's book was called anti-family and anti-Christian and was also challenged because of "language and moral content." Removed from classroom, Miller, Missouri, 1980. Challenged frequently throughout the U.S.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

Bridge to Terabithia
By Katherine Paterson
Removed from the curriculum in Pulaski Township, Pennsylvania, in 1996. Two parents described the book as profane, disrespectful of adults, and said it created an elaborate fantasy work they felt might lead to confusion about reality.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

Carrie
By Stephen King
Considered "trash" that is especially harmful for "younger girls." Challenged by Clark High School library, Las Vegas, Nevada, 1975. Placed on special closed shelf in Union High School library, Vergennes, Vermont, 1978.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

Catch-22
By Joseph Heller
Considered "dangerous" because of objectionable language. Banned in Strongsville, Ohio, 1972 (overturned in 1976). Challenged by Dallas, Texas, Independent School District high school libraries, 1974,Snoqualmie, Washington, 1979.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Anti-war.

Catcher in the Rye
By J.D. Salinger
Challenged in schools in Florida, Maine, California, Georgia, and Tennessee. (ALA Database) Considered "dangerous" because of vulgarity, occultism, violence and sexual content. Bannings, removals and challenges: Freeport High School, DeFuniak Springs, Florida, 1985. Issaquah, Washington, optional high school reading list, 1978; Required reading list, Middleville, Michingan, 1979.; Jackson-Milton school libraries, North Jackson, Ohio, 1980; Anniston, Alabama, high school libraries, 1982. Challenged by Libby (Montana) High School, 1983. One 1983 attempt to ban the book simply gave "the book's contents" as the reason.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

The Chocolate War
By Robert Cormier
This book was removed from middle school libraries in the Riverside, California, Unified School District in 1996 after a district committee decided the book was inappropriate for seventh and eighth graders to read without class discussion. The book was retained in the East Stroudsburg, Pennsylvania, ninth-grade currculum after students, parents and teachers spoke out in its favor.

A Clockwork Orange
By Anthony Burgess
"Objectionable" language. Removed from: Westport, Rhode Island, high school classrooms, 1977; Aurora, Colorado, high school classrooms, 1976; Anniston, Alabama, high school libraries, 1982.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry, Violence.

The Color Purple
By Alice Walker
Retained as an English course reading assignment in the Junction City, Oreg. high school (1995) after a challenge to Walker's Pulitzer Prize-winning novel caused months of controversy. Although an alternative reading assignment was available, the book was challenged due to "inappropriate language, graphic sexual scenes, and book's negative of black men." Considered inappropriate because of its "troubling ideas about race relations, man's relationship to God, African history and human sexuality." Challenged by Oakland, California, high school honors class, 1984; rejected for purchase by Hayward, California, school trustees.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry, Race and Ethnicity, Women.

The Crucible
By Arthur Miller
This playwright's classic parable is really about the anti-communist witch hunts of McCarthyism. Considered dangerous because it contains "sick words from the mouths of demon-possessed people." Challenged by Cumberland Valley High School, Harrisburg, Pennsylvania, 1982.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Anti-communism, Fiction & Poetry.

Cujo
By Stephen King
Profanity and strong sexual content cited as reasons for opposition. Banned by Washington County, Alabama, Board of Education, 1985; challenged by Rankin County, Mississippi, School District, 1984; removed from Bradford, New York, school library, 1985; rejected for purchase by Hayward, California, school trustees, 1985.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

The Day No Pigs Will Die
By Robert Newton Peck
Pulled in 1995 from middle schools in Anderson, South Carolina, after a parent asked that the book be removed from reading lists due to graphic, gory descriptions and scenes of brutality (the descriptions of two pigs mating, a pig being slaughtered, and a cow giving birth.) While a review committee recommended retaining the story of a Shaker boy coming of age in rural Vermont, the Board of Trustees voted to remove the book.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Children's Books.

Death Of A Salesman
By Arthur Miller
A powerful commentary on the downside of the American dream. Cited for profanity. Banned by Spring Valley Community High School, French Lick, Indiana, 1981; Challenged by Dallas, Texas, Independent School District high school libraries, 1974.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

The Devil's Alternate
By Frederick Forsyth
Removed by Evergreen School District, Vancouver, Washington, 1983.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

The Diary of Anne Frank
By Anne Frank
The true story, told in her own words, of just one victim of the Nazi holocaust against the Jews. Various reasons have been used to justify a number of attempts to ban Anne Frank's diary. In one 1983 incident, four members of the Alabama State Textbook Committee called for its rejection because it was a "real downer." Challenged by Wise County, Virginia, 1982 for objections to sexually offensive passages.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Race and Ethnicity.

East Of Eden
By John Steinbeck
Considered "ungodly and obscene." Removed from Anniston, Alabama, high school libraries, 1982; Morris, Manitoba, school libraries, 1982.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

A Farewell To Arms
By Ernest Hemingway
Labeled as a "sex novel." Challenged by Dallas, Texas, Independent School District high school libraries, 1974; Vernon-Verona-Sherill, New York, School District, 1980.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

Firestarter
By Stephen King
Cited for "graphic descriptions of sexual acts, vulgar language and violence." Challenged by Campbell County, Wyoming, school system, 1983-1984.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

Flowers For Algernon
By Daniel Keyes
A poignant, haunting science-fiction novel about a mentally retarded man who becomes a genius through an experimental medical procedure. Explicit, distasteful love scenes cited among reasons for opposition. Banned by Plant City, Florida, 1976; Emporium, Pennsylvania, 1977; Glen Rose (Arkansas) High School library, 1981. Challenged by Oberlin (Ohio) High School, 1983; Glenrock (Wyoming) High School, 1984.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

Flowers In The Attic
By V.C. Andrews
Considered "dangerous" because it contains "offensive passages concerning incest and sexual intercourse." Challenged by Richmond (Rhode Island) High School, 1983.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

Forever
By Judy Blume
In the Wilton, Iowa, School District, a parent complained in 1996 after her 13 year-old daughter read the book. Detractors cite its "four-letter words and [talk] about masturbation, birth control and disobedience to parents."Removed from Mediapolis, Iowa school district libraries (1994) because it "does not promote abstinence and monogamous relationships [and] lacks any aethetic, literary or social value." Returned to the shelves a month later but accessible only to high school students. Also challenged by: Midvalley Junior-Senior High School library, Scranton, Pennsylvania, 1982; Orlando, Florida, schools, 1982; Akron, Ohio, School District libraries, 1983; Howard-Suamico (Wisconsin) High School, 1983; Holdredge, Nebraska, Public Library, 1984; Cedar Rapids, Iowa, Public Library, 1984; Patrick County, Virginia, School Board, 1986; Park Hill (Missouri) South Junior High School library,1982.

Funhouse
By Dean Koontz
Removed from the South Brunswick Middle School Library in Boiling Spring Lakes, N.C. (1995) by a patron because the book "contains material on orgies, rape and lesbianism. There is also blasphemy and the book promotes domestic violence and alcohol abuse." The book was donated by the Lions Club in a book drive.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

George's Marvelous Medicine
By Roald Dahl
Challenged at the Stafford County, Va. Schools (1995) because the book "posed a safety threat because the boy in the story warms household items, such as paint thinner and soap to make a potion."
Other Topics Related to This Book: Children's Books, Fiction & Poetry.

The Grapes of Wrath
By John Steinbeck
An epic novel of social injustice during the Depression, The Grapes of Wrath has been censored countless times, Considered "dangerous" because of obscene language and the unfavorable depiction of a former minister. First burned by the St. Louis public library in 1939. Banned in Kanawha, Iowa, 1980; Morris, Manitoba, 1982. Challenged by Vernon-Verona-Sherill, New York, School District, 1980; Challenged by Richford, Vermonth, 1991. Challenged in 1989 in an 11th grade English class in Westwood, California.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry, Food/Farming, Labor/Union.

Harriet the Spy
By Louise Fitzhugh
While Harriet the Spy is a favorite of many, it was challenged in the Xenia, Ohio, school libraries in 1983 because the book "teaches children to lie, spy, back-talk and curse."
Other Topics Related to This Book: Children's Books.

I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings
By Maya Angelou
An eloquent, acclaimed work of autobiographical fiction dealing with the issues of racism, violence and sexual abuse of children. In her book, Angelou describes being raped as a child by her mother's boyfriend. This book has been repeatedly subjected to attempts at censorship. In Caddo, Alabama, a parent filed a complaint charging that the book contains things that the adolescent mind does not need to be subjected to. Also challenged at the Carrol School in Southlake, Tex (1995) because it was deemed "pornographic" and full of "gross evils." Considered "dangerous" because it preaches "bitterness and hatred against whites," it was also challenged by the Alabama State Textbook Committee in 1983.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry, Race and Ethnicity, Sexual Abuse, Violence.

Iggie's House
By Judy Blume

The Indian in the Cupboard
By Lynne Reid Banks
Removed from the Bemidji, Minn. school district voluntary reading list and from the school library shelves (1995) because it contains subtle stereotypes inconsistent with district diversity goals.

By Norma Klein

Has been challenged in both school and public libraries in Florida, New Jersey, New York and Washington.

Lady Chatterly's Lover
By D. H. Lawrence
D.H. Lawrence's classic work about a married woman's affair was declared an "obscene and filthy work" that could not be sent through the mail by the U.S. Postmaster General in 1959. The courts disagreed, and it can now be shipped freely.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

By William C. Bower
Considered "dangerous" because it is "a perverted commentary on the King James Version." Burned in Gastonia, North Carolina, 1986.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Religion.

By William Golding
Considered "demoralizing inasmuch as it implies that man is little more than an animal." Challenged by Dallas, Texas, Independent School District high school libraries, 1974; Sully Buttes (South Dakota) High School, 1981; Owen (North Carolina) High School, 1981; Marana (Arizona) High School, 1983; Olney, Texas, Independent School District, 1984.

The Martian Chronicles
By Ray Bradbury
A haunting collection of short stories imagining human encounters with intelligent life on Mars. Profanity and the use of God's name in vain sparked opposition to this novel. Challenged by Haines City (Florida) High School, 1982.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

The Matarese Circle
By Robert Ludlum
"Unnecessarily rough language and sexual descriptions" caused opposition to this novel. Restricted (to students with parental consent) by Pierce (Nebraska) High School, 1983.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

My Brother Sam is Dead
By James Lincoln Collier; Christopher Collier
A student's grandmother attempted to have this Newberry-Award winning book, by James Lincoln Collier and Christopher Collier, removed from a Lakewood, Colorado, elementary school. She cited the book's language and references to rape, drinking and battlefield violence.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Children's Books.

My Teacher is an Alien
By Bruce Coville
Challenged in the Elizabethtown, Pa. schools (1994) because it demeans teachers and parents as dumb, and portrays the main character as handling a problem on her own, rather than relying on the help of others.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Children's Books, Fiction & Poetry.

Nineteen Eighty-Four
By George Orwell
Although Orwell was a socialist, this nightmare vision of totalitarian society has often been appropriated as an anti-Communist parable, but has sometimes drawn objections from people who find it pro-Communist and sexually explicit. Challenged by Jackson County, Florida, 1981.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

Of Mice And Men
By John Steinbeck
A tragic tale of the friendship between two drifters, one of whom is mentally retarded. Considered "dangerous" because of its profanity and "vulgar language." Banned in: Syracuse, Indiana, 1974; Oil City, Pennsylvania, 1977; Grand Blanc, Michigan, 1979; Continental, Ohio, 1980; Skyline High School, Scottsboro, Alabama, 1983. Challenged by Greenville, South Carolina, 1977; Vernon-Verona- Sherill, New York, School District, 1980; St. David, Arizona, 1981; Telly City, Indiana, 1982; Knoxville, Tennessee, School Board, 1984.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

One Day In The Life Of Ivan Denisovich
By Alexander Solzhenitsyn
A classic, harrowing story about hardships and injustice in a Soviet prison camp. Objectionable language. Removed by Milton (New Hampshire) High School library, 1976. Challenged by Mahwah, New Jersey, 1976; Challenged by Omak, Washington, 1979; Challenged by Mohawk Trail Regional High School, Buckland, Mass, 1981.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Anti-communism, Fiction & Poetry, Human RIghts.

One Flew Over The Cuckoo's Nest
By Ken Kesey
This brilliant novel about a charismatic rebel trapped in a mental institution was written as a parable about the erosion of human freedom and individuality in modern society. Removed from required reading list by Westport, Massachusetts, 1977. Banned by Freemont High School, St. Anthony, Idaho. (Instructor was fired.) Challenged by Merrimack (New Hampshire) High School, 1982.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

By The Boston Women's Health Collective
This highly respected women's health manual has been removed from school libraries on numerous occasions. Reasons vary, but it has been called "pornographic" and "filthy," and was removed from the public library in Three Rivers, Michigan because "it promotes homosexuality and perversion."

Slaughterhouse-Five
By Kurt Vonnegut, Jr
A stylistically innovative tale about a man lost in time, this book was based in part on Vonnegut's experiences as a German prisoner of war who survived the U.S. firebombing of Dresden, and contains graphic depictions of the absurdity and horrors of war. Considered "dangerous" because of violent, irreverent, profane and sexually explicit content. Burned in Drake, North Carolina, 1973; Rochester, Michigan, 1972; Levittown, New York, 1975; North Jackson, Ohio, 1979; Lakeland, Florida, 1982. Barred from purchase by Washington Park High School, Racine, Wi, 1984. Challenged by Owensboro (Kentucky) High School library, 1985.

Superfudge

Tarzan of the Apes
By Edgar Rice Burroughs
This classic story of a man raised in the wild, which later became the basis for a comic book series and many movies, was removed from the Los Angeles Public Library because Tarzan was "living in sin" with Jane. NOTE: Tarzan has certainly been popular through the years, but readers should be aware that the book is strongly colored by the racial prejudices which were taken for granted when Burroughs was writing.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry, Race and Ethnicity.

By S.E. Hinton
Objections to "graphic language, subject matter, immoral tone and lack of literary quality." Challenged by Pagosa Springs, Colorado, 1983.

To Kill A Mockingbird
By Harper Lee
Considered "dangerous" because of profanity and undermining of race relations. Challenges: Eden Valley, Minnesota, 1977 (temporarily banned); Vernon-Verona-Sherill, New York, School District, 1980; Warren, Indiana, township schools, 1981; Waukegan, Illinois, School District, 1984; Kansas City, Missouri, junior high schools, 1985; Park Hill (Missouri) Junior High School, 1985. Protested by black parents and NAACP in Casa Grande (Arizona) Elementary School District, 1985.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry, Race and Ethnicity.

Ulysses
By James Joyce
James Joyce's stream-of-consciousness novel about a day in the internal and external life of an average Dubliner is now known to be a classic, but the U.S. Customs Service banned its importation into this country for 15 years on the grounds that it was obscene. In 1933, the ACLU won a major legal victory that forced the U.S. Customs Service to lift its ban. "Given its long history of censorship, Ulysses has rarely been selected for high school libraries." -- Judith Krug, director, Office for Intellectual Freedom, American Library Association, 1986.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

Uncle Tom's Cabin
By Harriet Beecher Stowe
This sentimental novel about the horrors of slavery became a rallying symbol for the abolition movement. Ironically, its use of the word "nigger" caused opposition in recent years. Challenged by Waukegan, Illinois, School District, 1984.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry, Race and Ethnicity.

Webster's Ninth New Collegiate Dictionary
By The Merriam-Webster Editorial Staff
Dictionaries get banned, too! Webster's Ninth New Collegiate Dictionary was removed from classrooms in Carlsbad, New Mexico, because it defines "obscene" words.

Welcome to the Monkey House
By Kurt Vonnegut
An Alabama teacher was dismissed in 1970 for assigning this collection of short stories to her 11th grade English class, because the book promoted "the killing off of elderly people and free sex."
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

Where The Sidewalk Ends
By Shel Silverstein
Considered by opponents to undermine parental, school and religious authority. Pulled from shelves for review by Minot, North Dakota, public school libraries, 1986. Challenged by Xenia, Ohio, school libraries, 1983.

Where's Waldo?
By Martin Hanford
The goal in this ubiquitous children's picture book is to find Waldo in a crowd of tiny, colorful figures. While some people may have trouble finding Waldo, others think they've found something else. In 1989, the book was attacked in the Saginaw, Michigan, libraries because "on some of the pages there are dirty things."
Other Topics Related to This Book: Children's Books.
