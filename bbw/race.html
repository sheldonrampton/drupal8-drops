Race and Ethnicity


The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn
By Mark Twain
Considered by some to be the first great American novel, this is undoubtedly Mark Twain's finest work, a bitingly humorous satire that strongly criticized the southern institution of slavery. Ironically, in recent years it has been occasionally labeled itself as a racist book. In 1996 in Tempe, Arizona, it was challenged in the Union High School District after a parent requested that all books containing expressions that could be offensive to minority students be removed from school reading lists. Removed from the required reading lists in East San Jose, Calif. high schools (1995) in response to objections raised by African-American parents. They said the book's use of racial epithets, including the frequent use of word "nigger," erodes their children's self esteem and affects their performance in school. Considered "dangerous" because of objectionable language and "racist" terms and content. Challenged by Winnetka, Illinois, 1976; Warrington, Pennsylvania, 1981; Davenport, Iowa, 1981; Fairfax County, Virginia, 1982; Houston, Texas, 1982; State College, Pennsylvania, area school district 1983; Springfield, Illinois, 1983 Waukegan, Illinois, 1984.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Fiction & Poetry.

Black Boy
By Richard Wright
Richard Wright's pathbreaking book about the black experience in the U.S. was removed, along with nine other titles, by Island Trees, New York, schools in 1976. The books were considered "immoral, anti-American, anti-Christian, or just plain filthy." The restriction was lifted by a ruling from the U.S. Supreme Court in 1982.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Banned Books, Fiction & Poetry.

Color Conscious
By K. Anthony Appiah; Amy Gutmann
Appiah, Harvard Professor of Afro-American Studies, and his co-author, Amy Gutmann seek to clear the ground for a discussion of the place of race in politics and in our moral lives. Writing candidly, Appiah argues the need to balance carefully the calls of race against the many other dimensions of individual identity, and suggests what this might mean for our political life.

The Color Purple
By Alice Walker
Retained as an English course reading assignment in the Junction City, Oreg. high school (1995) after a challenge to Walker's Pulitzer Prize-winning novel caused months of controversy. Although an alternative reading assignment was available, the book was challenged due to "inappropriate language, graphic sexual scenes, and book's negative of black men." Considered inappropriate because of its "troubling ideas about race relations, man's relationship to God, African history and human sexuality." Challenged by Oakland, California, high school honors class, 1984; rejected for purchase by Hayward, California, school trustees.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Banned Books, Fiction & Poetry, Women.

Color-Blind
By Ellis Cose
The author of THE RAGE OF THE PRIVILEGED CLASS explores the racial transformation of this nation and offers a creative and viable twelve-step blueprint for the development of a race-neutral society.

The Diary of Anne Frank
By Anne Frank
The true story, told in her own words, of just one victim of the Nazi holocaust against the Jews. Various reasons have been used to justify a number of attempts to ban Anne Frank's diary. In one 1983 incident, four members of the Alabama State Textbook Committee called for its rejection because it was a "real downer." Challenged by Wise County, Virginia, 1982 for objections to sexually offensive passages.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Banned Books.

I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings
By Maya Angelou
An eloquent, acclaimed work of autobiographical fiction dealing with the issues of racism, violence and sexual abuse of children. In her book, Angelou describes being raped as a child by her mother's boyfriend. This book has been repeatedly subjected to attempts at censorship. In Caddo, Alabama, a parent filed a complaint charging that the book contains things that the adolescent mind does not need to be subjected to. Also challenged at the Carrol School in Southlake, Tex (1995) because it was deemed "pornographic" and full of "gross evils." Considered "dangerous" because it preaches "bitterness and hatred against whites," it was also challenged by the Alabama State Textbook Committee in 1983.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Banned Books, Fiction & Poetry, Sexual Abuse, Violence.

The Indian in the Cupboard
By Lynne Reid Banks
Removed from the Bemidji, Minn. school district voluntary reading list and from the school library shelves (1995) because it contains subtle stereotypes inconsistent with district diversity goals.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Banned Books, Children's Books.

Race, Crime, and the Law
By Randall Kennedy
Harvard Law School professor and former law clerk to Thurgood Marshall, Kennedy offers a comprehensive and provocative examination of the crossroads at which race relations in America intersect with the criminal justice system.

The Suitcase
Refugee Voices from Bosnia & Croatia
By Julie Mertus, Editor; Jasmina Tesanovic, Editor; Habiba Metikos, Editor
THE SUITCASE gives voice to a group who are often talked about but seldom listened to: the people, mostly women, who were displaced by the war in the Balkans.
Other Topics Related to This Book: War.

Tarzan of the Apes
By Edgar Rice Burroughs
This classic story of a man raised in the wild, which later became the basis for a comic book series and many movies, was removed from the Los Angeles Public Library because Tarzan was "living in sin" with Jane. NOTE: Tarzan has certainly been popular through the years, but readers should be aware that the book is strongly colored by the racial prejudices which were taken for granted when Burroughs was writing.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Banned Books, Fiction & Poetry.

To Kill A Mockingbird
By Harper Lee
Considered "dangerous" because of profanity and undermining of race relations. Challenges: Eden Valley, Minnesota, 1977 (temporarily banned); Vernon-Verona-Sherill, New York, School District, 1980; Warren, Indiana, township schools, 1981; Waukegan, Illinois, School District, 1984; Kansas City, Missouri, junior high schools, 1985; Park Hill (Missouri) Junior High School, 1985. Protested by black parents and NAACP in Casa Grande (Arizona) Elementary School District, 1985.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Banned Books, Fiction & Poetry.

Uncle Tom's Cabin
By Harriet Beecher Stowe
This sentimental novel about the horrors of slavery became a rallying symbol for the abolition movement. Ironically, its use of the word "nigger" caused opposition in recent years. Challenged by Waukegan, Illinois, School District, 1984.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Banned Books, Fiction & Poetry.

The Warrior's Honor
Ethnic War and the Modern Conscience
By Michael Ignatieff
Michael Ignatieff is a London-based reporter who has spent the last decade traveling between the civil wars that have erupted in Europe and Africa at the end of this century. In his newest book, Ignatieff continues his exploration of the causes of ethnic conflict in these regions, examining how they began, how they escalated into genocidal war, and how the West has viewed and dealt with them. He looks at the paradox of modern media coverage of war, which at the height of conflict can provoke a global empathy unique to this era, yet is too fickle and inconsistent to keep our attention on any one conflict for long. He travels with Boutros Boutros-Ghali around Africa, stopping in Rwanda, Zaire, and Angola, and learns from him the political frustrations that have all but immobilized the U.N. in its ability to deal with these wars the way many in the West think they should be addressed. He also offers a history of the Red Cross, which follows the organization from its birth in 1864 to modern day, using this topic as a point of departure, like all of the essays in this book, to analyze the ever-changing state of modern warfare, ethnic conflict, how we view them now, and how we need to begin looking at them in the future.
Other Topics Related to This Book: War.

White Lies, White Power
The Fight Against White Supremacy and Reactionary Violence
"This powerful book explores the lasting power of racism in daily life. Novick places most of the blame on the political and economic system that the U.S. has firmly in place. While racist groups, such as the Ku Klux Klan, can attract attention (and new adherents) by making easy scapegoats of certain peoples, Novick argues it would be wrong to think of such groups as representing a 'fringe.' They exist precisely because they reflect feelings deeply held by the society and the state." You'll get your money's worth from buying this book if you just read the chapter on the Los Angeles Police Department.
Other Topics Related to This Book: Human Rights.
